The behavior of waves in plasmas is characterised by Maxwell's Equations in 2 field form;
$\displaystyle \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho_f}{\epsilon} $
$\displaystyle \nabla\cdot \mathbf{B} = 0 $
$\displaystyle \nabla x \mathbf{E} = - \frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} $
$\displaystyle \nabla x \mathbf{B} = u\mathbf{J}_c + $ $\displaystyle u\epsilon \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} $
For mathematical ease, we also assume the wave to be complex since this drastically reduces computation via the following;
$\displaystyle \nabla \rightarrow i \mathbf{k} $
$\displaystyle \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \rightarrow i \omega $
We now consider a monochromatic plane wave, characterised by;
$\displaystyle \mathbf{E} (\mathbf{r} , t) = $ $\displaystyle \mathbf{E}_0 \exp{ i ( \mathbf{k \cdot r} - \omega t) }$
$\displaystyle \mathbf{B} (\mathbf{r} , t) = $ $\displaystyle \mathbf{B}_0 \exp{ i ( \mathbf{k \cdot r} - \omega t) } $
where the physical fields are given by the real parts.

$\displaystyle \frac{ d^2 y }{d x^2 } = \frac{n}{\Omega^2} \frac{dn}{dy} $

The dispersion relation of the plasma is given by; $\displaystyle k = \frac{\omega}{\Omega} ( 1 - \frac{\omega_p ^2}{\omega^2} ) ^ { \frac{1}{2} } $
